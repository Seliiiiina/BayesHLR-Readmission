\title{\textbf{Bayesian Hierarchical Logistic Regression for\\
       30-Day Hospital Readmission Among Diabetic Patients:\\
       The Role of Medical Specialty}}
\author{Selina You \and Zhouhan}
\date{March 2026}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Hospital readmission within 30 days is a costly and clinically important outcome.
We investigate whether medical specialty contributes to readmission risk
after adjusting for patient-level characteristics, using a Bayesian hierarchical
logistic regression model with specialty-specific random intercepts.
The model is fitted via a custom Metropolis-within-Gibbs sampler on a cohort
of 37,964 diabetic patient encounters across 19 specialty groups.
We find substantive between-specialty heterogeneity ($\hat{\tau} = 0.437$,
95\% CrI $[0.298, 0.644]$), with Physical Medicine and Rehabilitation showing
significantly elevated readmission risk.  Key patient-level predictors include
prior inpatient visits (OR $= 1.22$), comorbid neoplasms (OR $= 1.51$), and
A1C measurement (OR $= 1.42$).  Prior sensitivity analysis confirms robustness
of all conclusions.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Unplanned hospital readmissions within 30 days of discharge represent a
significant burden on healthcare systems, with estimated annual costs exceeding
\$26 billion in the United States alone.  For patients with diabetes---a
population characterised by high comorbidity and complex medication
regimens---readmission rates are particularly elevated.

Prior work has identified patient-level predictors of readmission (e.g., length
of stay, number of prior admissions, comorbidity burden), typically using
standard logistic regression.  However, an important structural feature is
often ignored: patients are treated by physicians from different medical
specialties, and these specialties may differ systematically in their
post-discharge care patterns, case mix beyond what is captured by available
covariates, or practice style.  Ignoring this clustering can lead to biased
standard errors and missed opportunities for targeted intervention.

\paragraph{Research question.}
\emph{Does specialty-level heterogeneity significantly contribute to 30-day
readmission risk among diabetic patients, after adjusting for patient-level
covariates?}

\paragraph{Why a Bayesian approach?}
A Bayesian hierarchical model offers three advantages over frequentist
alternatives for this problem:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item \textbf{Partial pooling.}  Small-sample specialties (e.g.,
    Physical Medicine, $n = 288$) borrow strength from larger groups through
    the shared hyperparameter $\tau$, yielding more stable estimates than
    either complete pooling (ignoring specialty) or no pooling (fixed effects).
  \item \textbf{Coherent uncertainty quantification.}  Posterior credible
    intervals for $\tau$ directly quantify our uncertainty about the
    \emph{magnitude} of between-specialty variation, rather than reducing
    this to a single $p$-value.
  \item \textbf{Principled model comparison.}  The Deviance Information
    Criterion (DIC) and posterior predictive checks provide model
    assessment tools that are native to the Bayesian framework.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Data and Cohort Definition}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Data Source}

We use the \emph{Diabetes 130-US Hospitals} dataset, which contains 101,766
inpatient encounters from 130 US hospitals over a 10-year period (1999--2008).
Each record includes patient demographics, admission details, diagnoses
(ICD-9 codes), laboratory results, and medication information.

\subsection{Cohort Construction}

Starting from $N_{\text{raw}} = 101{,}766$ encounters, we applied the
following inclusion criteria:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Remove duplicates and retain first encounter} per patient,
    yielding one independent observation per individual
    (removed 30,248 repeated encounters).
  \item \textbf{Exclude expired/hospice discharges} (removed 1,545 records)
    to focus on patients at risk for readmission.
  \item \textbf{Exclude unknown specialty} (removed 32,008 records, 45.7\%)
    since specialty group assignment is central to our research question.
\end{enumerate}
The resulting analytic cohort contains $N = 37{,}964$ encounters across
$J = 19$ specialty groups, with an overall 30-day readmission rate of 9.60\%.

\section{Model Specification}
\label{sec:model}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Likelihood}

Let $y_i \in \{0,1\}$ denote whether encounter $i$ resulted in 30-day
readmission.  Conditional on parameters, outcomes are independent Bernoulli:
\begin{equation}
  y_i \mid \alpha, \bbeta, \bu \;\ind\;
  \Bernoulli(\pi_i), \quad
  \pi_i = \frac{\exp(\eta_i)}{1 + \exp(\eta_i)}, \quad
  \eta_i = \alpha + \bx_i^\top \bbeta + u_{j[i]},
\end{equation}
where $\bx_i \in \mathbb{R}^{K}$ is the covariate vector ($K = 43$),
$j[i] \in \{1,\dots,J\}$ indicates the specialty group, and $u_{j[i]}$
is the specialty-specific random intercept.

The full likelihood is:
\begin{equation}
  p(\by \mid \alpha, \bbeta, \bu)
  = \prod_{i=1}^{N} \frac{\exp(y_i\,\eta_i)}{1 + \exp(\eta_i)}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Prior Distributions}

\begin{align}
  \alpha &\sim \Normal(0, \sigma_\alpha^2), && \sigma_\alpha = 5, \\
  \beta_k &\iid \Normal(0, s_\beta^2), && s_\beta = 2.5, \quad k = 1,\dots,K, \\
  u_j \mid \tau &\iid \Normal(0, \tau^2), && j = 1,\dots,J, \\
  \tau &\sim \HalfNormal(0, s_\tau^2), && s_\tau = 1.0.
\end{align}
The priors are weakly informative: $\sigma_\alpha = 5$ places the baseline
log-odds within a wide plausible range; $s_\beta = 2.5$ allows odds ratios up
to $\sim e^5 \approx 150$; $\tau \sim \HalfNormal(0,1)$ concentrates mass
near zero while permitting substantial heterogeneity.

\subsection{Hierarchical Structure}

The key feature is the exchangeable random intercepts
$u_j \mid \tau \iid \Normal(0, \tau^2)$.  The shared scale $\tau$ induces
\emph{partial pooling}: specialty estimates are shrunk toward the global mean,
with the degree of shrinkage inversely related to group size $n_j$.  When
$\tau \to 0$, all specialties collapse to a single intercept (complete
pooling); as $\tau \to \infty$, each group estimates its own intercept
independently (no pooling).

\subsection{Assumptions}
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item Conditional independence of outcomes given parameters.
  \item One observation per patient (first encounter retained).
  \item Specialty assignment is exogenous (not modelled).
  \item Only random intercepts; no random slopes.
  \item Prior independence: $\alpha \perp \bbeta \perp \tau$ \textit{a priori};
    $\bu$ depends on $\tau$ only.
\end{enumerate}

% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\section{Posterior Derivation}
\label{sec:derivation}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Joint Distribution and Posterior}

By Bayes' theorem:
\begin{equation}
  p(\alpha,\bbeta,\bu,\tau \mid \by, \bX)
  \;\propto\;
  p(\by \mid \alpha,\bbeta,\bu,\bX)\;
  p(\alpha)\;p(\bbeta)\;p(\bu\mid\tau)\;p(\tau).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Log-Posterior}

Substituting the likelihood and priors:
\begin{align}
  \ell(\alpha,\bbeta,\bu,\tau)
  &= \underbrace{\sum_{i=1}^{N}
    \bigl[y_i\,\eta_i - \log(1+e^{\eta_i})\bigr]}_{\text{(I) log-likelihood}}
  - \underbrace{\frac{\alpha^2}{2\sigma_\alpha^2}}_{\text{(II)}}
  - \underbrace{\frac{1}{2s_\beta^2}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\beta_k^2}_{\text{(III)}}
  \notag\\
  &\quad
  - \underbrace{J\log\tau + \frac{1}{2\tau^2}\sum_{j=1}^{J}u_j^2}_{\text{(IV)}}
  - \underbrace{\frac{\tau^2}{2s_\tau^2}}_{\text{(V)}}
  + \text{const.}
  \label{eq:logpost}
\end{align}

The logistic likelihood makes this posterior non-conjugate: no parameter block
has a closed-form full conditional.

\subsection{Full Conditional Distributions}

We derive the log-target $h(\cdot)$ for each parameter block by collecting
terms from~\eqref{eq:logpost} that depend on the parameter being updated.

\paragraph{Block 1: $\alpha$.}
\begin{equation}
  h(\alpha) = \sum_{i=1}^{N}
    \bigl[y_i\eta_i - \log(1+e^{\eta_i})\bigr]
  - \frac{\alpha^2}{2\sigma_\alpha^2}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Block 2: $\beta_k$ (for each $k = 1,\dots,K$).}
\begin{equation}
  h(\beta_k) = \sum_{i=1}^{N}
    \bigl[y_i\eta_i - \log(1+e^{\eta_i})\bigr]
  - \frac{\beta_k^2}{2s_\beta^2}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Block 3: $u_j$ (for each $j = 1,\dots,J$).}
Only group-$j$ observations contribute:
\begin{equation}
  h(u_j) = \sum_{i:\,j[i]=j}
    \bigl[y_i\eta_i - \log(1+e^{\eta_i})\bigr]
  - \frac{u_j^2}{2\tau^2}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Block 4: $\phi = \log\tau$ (reparametrised).}
Applying the change of variable $\tau = e^\phi$ with Jacobian $+\phi$:
\begin{equation}
  h(\phi) = -J\phi - \frac{1}{2e^{2\phi}}\sum_{j=1}^{J}u_j^2
            - \frac{e^{2\phi}}{2s_\tau^2} + \phi.
\end{equation}
Importantly, $h(\phi)$ does not involve the likelihood: data inform $\tau$
only indirectly through the posterior draws of $\bu$.

\subsection{Posterior Propriety}

\begin{proposition}
The posterior is proper.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
The Bernoulli likelihood is bounded ($0 \le p(\by \mid \cdot) \le 1$), and
all priors ($\alpha$, $\bbeta$, $\bu \mid \tau$, $\tau$) are proper
distributions.  Therefore the joint integrand is bounded above by the product
of proper priors, which integrates to 1.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Identifiability}

The intercept $\alpha$ and random intercepts $\bu$ are weakly
non-identifiable: shifting $\alpha$ by $+c$ and each $u_j$ by $-c$ leaves
$\eta_i$ unchanged.  The priors $p(\alpha)$ and $p(\bu \mid \tau)$ break this
symmetry by anchoring both at zero.  We further address this computationally
with a joint translation move (Block~5 in Section~\ref{sec:computation}).

% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\section{Computational Strategy}
\label{sec:computation}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Metropolis-within-Gibbs Sampler}

The non-conjugate logistic likelihood rules out Gibbs sampling with
closed-form conditionals.  Full-dimensional Metropolis--Hastings on the
$(1 + K + J + 1) = 64$-dimensional space would have prohibitively low
acceptance rates.  We therefore use \textbf{Metropolis-within-Gibbs (MwG)},
which decomposes the posterior into manageable blocks updated sequentially.

Each block uses a symmetric random walk proposal
$\theta^* = \theta^{(t)} + \epsilon$, $\epsilon \sim \Normal(0,
\sigma_{\text{prop}}^2)$, so the Hastings ratio cancels and the acceptance
probability reduces to $\min(1, \exp[h(\theta^*) - h(\theta^{(t)})])$.

\subsection{Block Update Schedule}

One full iteration proceeds as:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Block 1:} Update $\alpha$. Cost: $O(N)$ via incremental $\eta$ update.
  \item \textbf{Block 2:} For $k = 1,\dots,43$: update $\beta_k$.
    Cost: $O(N)$ per coordinate (incremental $\eta$ shift by $\Delta\beta_k \cdot X_{ik}$).
  \item \textbf{Block 3:} For $j = 1,\dots,19$: update $u_j$.
    Cost: $O(n_j)$ per group (only group-$j$ observations evaluated).
  \item \textbf{Block 4:} Update $\phi = \log\tau$. Cost: $O(J)$.
  \item \textbf{Block 5:} Joint translation move ($\alpha,\bu$), repeated
    $R=10$ times. Propose $\delta \sim \Normal(0, \sigma_{\text{blk}}^2)$,
    set $\alpha^* = \alpha + \delta$, $u_j^* = u_j - \delta$.
    Since $\eta$ is invariant, the likelihood cancels and only priors
    appear in the acceptance ratio. Cost: $O(J)$ with no likelihood evaluation.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Proposal Tuning}

Proposal standard deviations are tuned automatically over 10 rounds of pilot
runs (2,000 iterations each on a subsample of $n = 20{,}000$).  A
multiplicative rule scales $\sigma_{\text{prop}}$ by $\times 1.5$ if the
acceptance rate exceeds 0.44, and by $\div 1.5$ if below.  Per-coordinate
proposal SDs are maintained for each $\beta_k$ and $u_j$ to accommodate
varying posterior curvatures.

Final acceptance rates: $\alpha$: 0.40, $\bbeta$ (mean): 0.43,
$\bu$ (mean): 0.43, $\log\tau$: 0.37, block: 0.45---all within the
recommended 0.20--0.50 range.

\subsection{Chain Configuration}

We run 4 independent chains with dispersed random initialisations:
30,000 iterations per chain, burn-in of 8,000, thinning every 5th sample,
yielding 4,400 post-burn-in samples per chain and 17,600 pooled samples.

\subsection{Effective Sample Size and Cross-Chain Convergence ($\hat{R}$)}

We assess convergence through two complementary diagnostics:
effective sample size (ESS) and the split-$\hat{R}$ statistic
\citep{gelman1992inference,vehtari2021rank}.
Split-$\hat{R}$ divides each of the four chains in half, yielding
eight half-chains, and compares between- vs.\ within-chain variance;
values close to 1.0 indicate that the chains have converged to a
common stationary distribution.

Table~\ref{tab:ess} summarises both diagnostics across parameter types.
All 64 parameters satisfy $\hat{R} < 1.05$, with the maximum
$\hat{R} = 1.013$ (for $\beta_{28}$), well below the
conventional threshold.  All parameters have mean ESS $> 100$;
most exceed 500.  The between-group scale $\tau$ has
ESS $\approx 2{,}412$ and $\hat{R} = 1.000$, indicating
excellent mixing for the key hyperparameter.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
The strongest patient-level predictor of readmission is \textbf{prior
inpatient visits} (OR $= 1.22$ per SD increase), reflecting a pattern of
recurrent hospitalisation.  \textbf{Comorbid neoplasms} carries the highest
odds ratio (OR $= 1.51$), suggesting that cancer as a secondary diagnosis
substantially elevates readmission risk.  Conversely, having \textbf{neoplasms
as the primary diagnosis} is protective (OR $= 0.60$), likely because these
patients are discharged to oncology follow-up pathways.

The A1C measurement indicator (OR $= 1.42$) likely reflects disease severity
rather than a causal effect of testing: sicker patients are more likely to have
A1C measured.  Notably, sex, race, and insulin use are \emph{not} significant
predictors after adjustment.

\subsection{Specialty-Level Heterogeneity}

\paragraph{Between-group scale $\tau$.}
The posterior mean of $\tau$ is 0.437 (SD $= 0.090$, 95\% CrI [0.298, 0.644]),
with $P(\tau > 0.1 \mid \by) = 1.0$ and $P(\tau > 0.2 \mid \by) = 1.0$.
This provides strong evidence that specialty-level heterogeneity persists after
adjusting for patient covariates.

\paragraph{Specialty random effects.}
Figure~\ref{fig:forest} displays the posterior random intercepts with 95\% CrI.
\textbf{Physical Medicine and Rehabilitation} (PMR) has a markedly elevated
random intercept ($\hat{u} = +1.32$, 95\% CrI [0.99, 1.67]), the only
specialty whose interval is entirely above zero.  At the other extreme,
\textbf{Surgery-Cardiovascular/Thoracic} has a significantly negative effect
($\hat{u} = -0.58$, 95\% CrI [$-0.99$, $-0.21$]).  Most other specialties
have credible intervals that include zero, indicating that after covariate
adjustment, their readmission rates are consistent with the overall average.

\section{Sensitivity Analysis}
\label{sec:sensitivity}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

We assess robustness to prior specification by re-running the sampler under
three alternative configurations:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{A1:} Wider fixed-effect prior ($s_\beta = 5.0$).
  \item \textbf{B1:} Wider hyperprior ($s_\tau = 2.5$, Half-Normal).
  \item \textbf{B2:} Heavy-tailed hyperprior ($s_\tau = 2.5$, Half-Cauchy).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Results.}
Figure~\ref{fig:sens-tau} shows that the posterior of $\tau$ is virtually
identical across all four configurations (means: 0.437, 0.437, 0.441, 0.440),
confirming that the data overwhelm the prior for this hyperparameter.
Figure~\ref{fig:sens-beta} demonstrates that key fixed effects (e.g.,
$\text{diag1\_Neoplasms}$, $\text{comorbid\_neoplasms}$, $\text{A1C\_measured}$)
are stable across prior specifications.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Summary of Findings}

Our analysis provides three main findings:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Specialty matters.}  The between-specialty standard deviation
    $\tau = 0.437$ (95\% CrI [0.298, 0.644]) demonstrates that medical
    specialty contributes meaningfully to readmission risk beyond what is
    explained by patient characteristics.

  \item \textbf{PMR is an outlier.}  Physical Medicine and Rehabilitation shows
    dramatically elevated readmission risk ($u = +1.32$, corresponding to a
    raw rate of 32.3\%), even after adjusting for covariates.  This may
    reflect the unique patient population (e.g., stroke rehabilitation
    patients with high medical complexity) and could represent a target for
    quality improvement.

  \item \textbf{Key patient-level risk factors.}  Prior inpatient visits,
    comorbid neoplasms, A1C measurement, age, and number of diagnoses are
    the strongest predictors, collectively painting a picture of
    \emph{medical complexity} as the primary driver of readmission.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Likelihood-Driven vs.\ Prior-Driven Behavior}

With $N = 37{,}964$ observations, the posteriors of fixed effects $\bbeta$ are
overwhelmingly data-driven: the sensitivity analysis confirms negligible
prior influence.  The random intercepts exhibit a gradient: large groups
(e.g., Internal Medicine, $n = 10{,}975$) are likelihood-driven, while small
groups (e.g., PMR, $n = 288$) experience meaningful shrinkage toward zero.
The hyperparameter $\tau$ is estimated primarily from the empirical spread
of the $u_j$'s, with $J = 19$ groups providing sufficient information.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Unknown specialty exclusion.}  We dropped 45.7\% of
    encounters with unrecorded specialty.  These patients may differ
    systematically from those with known specialty assignment, potentially
    introducing selection bias.

  \item \textbf{No random slopes.}  Our model assumes that covariate effects
    are constant across specialties.  In practice, the effect of (e.g.)
    comorbidity burden may vary by specialty; random slopes could capture this
    but would substantially increase model complexity.

  \item \textbf{Temporal and institutional confounding.}  The data span
    10 years across 130 hospitals, but we do not model hospital-level or
    temporal effects.  Specialty effects may partly reflect unmeasured
    hospital-level variation.

  \item \textbf{Observational design.}  Specialty assignment is not random;
    patients are referred based on clinical need.  The estimated specialty
    effects reflect residual confounding as well as genuine practice
    differences.

  \item \textbf{Moderate discriminative performance.}  AUC $= 0.641$ reflects
    the inherent difficulty of predicting readmission from administrative
    data alone.  Clinical notes and social determinants of health---unavailable
    in this dataset---would likely improve prediction.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Future Directions}

Extensions could include: (i) a crossed random effects model with both
specialty and hospital groupings; (ii) random slopes for key covariates;
(iii) a time-varying model to capture temporal trends in readmission;
(iv) incorporation of the ``unknown specialty'' group via a mixture model.
